# Local-Context Block — Spec + Worked Examples

**For:** copywriter (this is the per-city writing job, C3) + John.
**Covers:** plan section C3, the one part that can't be automated and the reason the pages survive a core update.

This file sets the bar. The two worked examples below are real and finished. Once John signs off on the city list, every city gets one block written to this standard. The dev supplies the raw inputs (latitude-derived winter months, cloud-cover note, typical UV); the copywriter turns them into a paragraph that reads like a local wrote it.

---

## The spec

**Length:** 120–200 words. A tight, specific paragraph beats a vague long one.

**Must contain, woven into prose (not a list):**

1. The actual months that city has weak or no vitamin D sun (from the dev's solar model).
2. How the city's typical cloud or climate changes the real-world picture versus the clean theoretical numbers. This is where most cities differ from each other.
3. One concrete, named local spot for getting midday sun: a well-known park, waterfront, hill, or open space a resident would actually recognize.

**Hard rule (from C6):** if a sentence would be true of any city, cut it. "Residents enjoy spending time outdoors" is filler. "The Mancunian rain means the theoretical window and the real one rarely match from November on" is not. Specifics are the strongest human signal; vagueness is the strongest AI tell.

**Voice:** same rules as the template copy. Answer-first isn't required here (it's a context block, not the opener), but a small point of view is welcome.

## Worked example 1 — Manchester (53.5°N)

> Manchester sits at 53.5 degrees north, which gives it a vitamin D winter from roughly October to March. For those months the midday sun never climbs high enough, so the window on the table effectively closes and diet or a supplement does the work. The bigger local catch is the cloud. Manchester is one of the wettest big cities in England, and a grey, fully overcast sky can cut the UVB by most of what the clear-sky numbers promise. The minutes on this page assume the sun is actually out, which here is the exception as much as the rule from late autumn. On a clear spring or summer midday, you've got good options for open sky: Heaton Park in the north is one of the largest municipal parks in Europe, Platt Fields in Fallowfield is flat and open, and the waterfront at Salford Quays gives you unobstructed sky away from tall buildings. The rule of thumb here: when the sun does break through between April and September, take it.

*(Word count: ~165. Real facts: latitude, winter range, rainfall reputation, three named real locations.)*

## Worked example 2 — Seattle (47.6°N)

> Seattle's vitamin D problem isn't really its latitude, it's the cloud. At 47.6 degrees north the sun does clear the threshold for a usable window most of the year, closing only from about November to February. The catch is that Seattle spends a huge share of the year under marine-layer overcast, and heavy cloud can strip out up to 90 percent of the UVB that the clean numbers assume. So the practical vitamin D season here is shorter than the solar math suggests: the reliable stretch is roughly late June through September, when the grey finally lifts. When it does, locals know to chase it. Green Lake gives you an open loop with sky on all sides, Gas Works Park sits high on the water with no tree cover, and Alki Beach faces southwest across Puget Sound for afternoon light. The honest version of Seattle sun advice: the window is real, but it's the clear days inside it that count, and there are fewer of those than anywhere else on this list.

*(Word count: ~175. Real facts: latitude, marine-layer cloud, summer-dry pattern, three named real locations.)*

## Production notes

- Pull the three required facts from the dev's per-city data before writing, so the months and cloud note match the table exactly. A local block that contradicts the table is worse than none.
- For cities where the cloud story is weak (drier continental cities like Calgary), lead the block with something else specific: extreme winter cold keeping people indoors during the only usable hours, very short winter days, snow glare, or a notable elevation. There's always a real local angle; find it.
- Where I'm not personally sure of a named park or waterfront, I'll verify it before writing rather than guess. Wrong local detail is the fastest way to read as an outsider, which is the opposite of the point.
