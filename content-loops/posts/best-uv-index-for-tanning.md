## Post content

If you're searching for the best UV index to tan by, you've probably gotten a number with no explanation attached. The UV that causes tanning is a different wavelength from the UV that makes vitamin D, and the levels where tanning happens fastest are also where you burn fastest. There is no UV index at which tanning is risk-free; a tan at any level is the skin's response to UV damage. The scale below clarifies what each band actually does.

**The direct answer:** At a UV index of 3-5 (the "moderate" band on the WHO scale), tanning occurs slowly while burn risk stays manageable for most skin types. At UV 6-7, both tanning speed and burn risk go up. At UV 8 and above, serious UV damage accumulates quickly. The scale helps clarify the tradeoffs, especially if vitamin D is the actual goal rather than a cosmetic tan.

## What the UV index measures

The UV index is a standardized daily measure from meteorological agencies of UV radiation intensity reaching the ground at solar noon. It's weighted toward the wavelengths that cause sunburn (UVB). The WHO, WMO, and UNEP collaborated on the current scale:

- **0-2 (Low):** Minimal UV; no protection needed for most people
- **3-5 (Moderate):** Protection recommended (hat, SPF); enough UVB is now present for vitamin D synthesis
- **6-7 (High):** Protection essential for all skin types
- **8-10 (Very high):** Unprotected exposure causes harm quickly
- **11+ (Extreme):** Minimize midday outdoor exposure

UV index 3 is also where meaningful vitamin D synthesis starts. Below 3, skin makes very little vitamin D regardless of how long you stay out. Above 3, both tanning and vitamin D production are possible, through separate mechanisms described below.

## How the UV index affects tanning, burn risk, and vitamin D

Burn times assume Fitzpatrick I-II (fair) skin with no sunscreen. People with darker skin burn more slowly but also tan and produce vitamin D at a slower rate.

| UV index | WHO band | Tanning pace | Approx. burn time (fair skin) | Vitamin D production |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Low | Negligible | 2+ hours | Negligible |
| 3-5 | Moderate | Slow | ~30-60 min | Real (UVB present) |
| 6-7 | High | Moderate | ~15-25 min | Good; burn risk rising sharply |
| 8-10 | Very high | Fast | ~10-15 min | High; unprotected window is short |
| 11+ | Extreme | Very fast | Under 10 min | High; only brief sessions make sense |

As UV rises, every column accelerates: tanning, vitamin D synthesis, and burns. The gap between "made a useful vitamin D dose" and "now you're burning" shrinks as UV climbs. At UV 9 or 10, that window is 5-10 minutes for fair skin.

## UVA tans you. UVB makes vitamin D. They are not the same.

Tanning articles rarely separate these two, and the distinction changes how you read the UV index.

The UV reaching the ground is a blend of two types. UVA (roughly 320-400nm) is the dominant UV at ground level and the primary driver of tanning. It oxidizes existing melanin (the darkening you notice within an hour or two) and triggers new melanin production over the following days. UVA penetrates deeper into skin than UVB, causes photoaging (wrinkles, texture changes, age spots), and contributes to skin cancer. It also passes through glass, which is why you can tan through a car window.

UVB (roughly 280-315nm) causes sunburn and drives vitamin D synthesis. When UVB strikes skin, it converts 7-dehydrocholesterol (a compound naturally present in skin cells) into previtamin D3, which the body then converts to vitamin D3. UVB doesn't pass through glass; a desk by a sunny window produces no vitamin D despite the warmth and brightness. The UV index is primarily a UVB intensity measure.

So: a cosmetic tan is driven mainly by UVA, while vitamin D synthesis requires UVB. Both wavelengths arrive together in direct outdoor sunlight, so both processes run simultaneously outside. A tanning bed (typically UVA-dominant) produces a tan with very little vitamin D. A sunny window produces UVA tanning and photoaging with no real UVB, so no vitamin D either.

## Does a tan mean I got vitamin D?

Not by itself. A tan confirms UVA exposure; vitamin D requires UVB. In direct outdoor sun, both wavelengths arrive together, so you probably made some vitamin D while tanning. But tan depth doesn't measure how much D was produced, and you can develop a significant tan through glass or in a tanning bed without making vitamin D.

A tan is skin's response to UV-induced DNA damage: the skin produces more melanin as a buffer against further injury, not a sign the skin is thriving. The Skin Cancer Foundation is explicit on this point.

This matters for vitamin D reasoning. Trying to get more D by tanning more deeply doesn't work: synthesis plateaus once enough previtamin D3 has accumulated, and extra UV exposure adds damage with no extra D. The absence of a visible tan doesn't mean much either; fair skin makes vitamin D quickly at UV 5 or higher with minimal visible tanning.

## Is UV index 5 good for tanning?

UV 5 sits at the top of the "moderate" band. At this level, tanning happens slowly for fair to medium skin, with a burn window of roughly 30-60 minutes for Fitzpatrick I-II. It's also the range where vitamin D synthesis is real without the compressed timeframes of UV 8+.

There's nothing uniquely favorable about UV 5 for a cosmetic tan; UV 7 tans faster but also burns faster. Tanning speed and burn risk scale together and you can't separate them by choosing a UV number. What UV 5 offers is a slightly wider margin between the productive vitamin D window and the start of burn damage, which is why it comes up in discussions of timed sun exposure.

## What UV index is too high?

The WHO recommends sun protection starting at UV 3, the same threshold where vitamin D synthesis begins. That overlap isn't a coincidence. The conditions that make vitamin D synthesis possible are exactly the conditions that allow skin damage to accumulate.

At UV 8+, fair skin without sunscreen burns in 10-15 minutes. For a short vitamin D session, that's a narrow margin. At UV 11+, even medium skin types are at real risk in under 20 minutes without protection.

What counts as "too high" depends on what you're doing. UV 8+ compresses the vitamin D window enough that a timer is basically required. For longer outdoor time, UV 6+ is where sunscreen and a hat earn their keep. If you have fair skin or a skin cancer history, the math gets stricter: any unprotected session above UV 3 should be timed.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is UV index 5 good for tanning?**

At UV 5, tanning happens slowly for fair to medium skin. You'll see some tan develop over a 30-60 minute session without sunscreen, and you'll also be synthesizing vitamin D. Burn risk is lower than at UV 7+. There's no objective reason UV 5 is the "best" UV for a tan; faster tanning always comes with faster burning. If vitamin D is the actual goal rather than a cosmetic tan, UV 5 is a reasonable daily window to aim for.

**Can you tan and get vitamin D at the same time?**

Yes. Direct outdoor sunlight delivers both UVA (tanning) and UVB (vitamin D synthesis) simultaneously. Vitamin D accumulates faster: a useful dose can be synthesized in 10-30 minutes depending on skin type and UV, while a visible tan develops over hours and days. The two are driven by different wavelengths and don't track each other closely; depth of tan is not a measure of vitamin D production.

**Does a tan mean you got vitamin D?**

Not by itself. A tan confirms UVA exposure; vitamin D synthesis requires UVB. In direct outdoor sun both are present, so some vitamin D was probably made alongside the tan. But the degree of tanning doesn't measure how much, and you can develop a significant tan from a tanning bed or through glass without making vitamin D, since both sources are UVA-heavy with little UVB.

**What UV index is too high to go outside?**

There's no hard cutoff, but the WHO puts UV 8-10 in the "very high" band and UV 11+ in "extreme," recommending minimized unprotected midday exposure at both levels. At UV 9, fair skin without sunscreen can burn in 10-12 minutes, which is shorter than most people's intended vitamin D session. SPF 30+ and a hat extend the safe window substantially.

**Is tanning ever healthy?**

The evidence doesn't support it as a health practice. The Skin Cancer Foundation describes a tan as the skin's response to UV-induced DNA damage: a protective reaction, not a sign of health. No clinical guidelines recommend maintaining a cosmetic tan, and the long-term risks (photoaging, elevated skin cancer risk) are dose-dependent. Short, purposeful sun sessions for vitamin D are a different category: the goal is a specific dose, not accumulating pigment. The difference is intent and duration; a timed 15-minute session for vitamin D and lying out for two hours are not the same exposure.
