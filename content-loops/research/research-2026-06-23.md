## Strategic note: how to attack Pillar 2 without losing to the authority wall

Pillar 2 is the largest untapped vitamin-D search volume we have. It is also where we are most likely to waste a post if we aim at the wrong query. The head terms break down into two failure modes:

- **Authority wall.** `vitamin D deficiency symptoms`, `vitamin D deficiency treatment`, `low vitamin D` — page one is Cleveland Clinic, Yale Medicine, Healthline, WebMD, StatPearls/NIH, Merck. A landing-page blog does not displace these on a YMYL head term. (Verified SERP, 06-23.)
- **Wrong answer for our funnel.** The honest answer to most pure-deficiency queries is _"get tested, and for moderate-to-severe deficiency you likely need a supplement, not just sun."_ That is correct and we must say it — but a post whose payoff is "take a pill" is a weak install driver for a sun-timing app.

**The rankable, on-thesis slice is the intersection of deficiency and _sunlight_.** When the query already ties low vitamin D to _not enough sun_, three things line up at once:

1. The SERP drops to regional-hospital blogs, light-therapy sellers, and content farms (no NIH/Mayo wall on these exact phrasings).
2. The honest answer legitimately centers on _how much sun, when, and for whom_ — which is Bask's exact value.
3. The audience self-selects as people who suspect their indoor/low-sun lifestyle is the cause — high install intent.

The honest-framing discipline still binds hard here (YMYL): every Pillar 2 post must (a) say plainly that symptoms are non-specific and the only way to know is a blood test, (b) tell readers to consult a clinician, and (c) **not** claim sun alone fixes a real deficiency — for many people (darker skin, northern latitude, indoor work, winter) it cannot, and supplements are the right call. That honesty is the rankable, trust-earning gap versus the "just stand in the sun" content farms — and it's still on-thesis: Bask's job is to help you get the _right_ dose of sun, and to know when sun isn't enough.

## Scoped topics — Pillar 2 (DO NOT FRONT-LOAD; queue for Aug+ post-summer cycle)

### P2.1 — How long does it take to fix a vitamin D deficiency? (sun vs. supplements)

- **Intent / query cluster:** _how long does it take to correct vitamin D deficiency · how long to raise vitamin D levels · how long does it take for vitamin D to work · how long to fix low vitamin D with sun._ Searchers have just been told they're low and want a timeline.
- **Primary keyword:** how long to correct vitamin D deficiency
- **Secondaries:** how long does it take to raise vitamin D levels · how long for vitamin D to work · can you fix vitamin D deficiency with sun
- **Why we can rank:** SERP is commercial/supplement blogs (remedysnutrition, getmorevits, singlecare) plus AdventHealth/UCLA Health blog tier — **no hard NIH/Mayo wall on the "how long" phrasing.** A precisely-sourced, honest timeline beats the vague affiliate pages and is highly LLM-citable (clean numeric answer).
- **SEO/GEO:** Question-style H2s; a liftable direct-answer ("Most people restore healthy levels in **6–8 weeks**; someone starting near 15 ng/mL typically reaches 30+ ng/mL in ~4–6 weeks — but the timeline depends on starting level, dose, skin tone, latitude, and body weight"). FAQPage schema. A comparison block: _sun timeline vs. supplement timeline._
- **Honest framing / Bask fit:** The honest answer is "it depends on the variables" — which is the whole reason a generic minutes-number is useless and a tracker isn't. Bask = hitting a consistent daily sun dose for your skin/UV so the timeline is real, while being explicit that severe deficiency needs a clinician + likely supplements (we are not anti-supplement; we are pro-correct-dose).
- **Must-cite / verify:** 6–8-week restoration timeline and the 15→30 ng/mL window — corroborate against a clinical source (UCLA Health blog and AdventHealth state it; **upgrade to a peer-reviewed pharmacokinetics/repletion source before publishing** rather than leaning on supplement blogs). NIH ODS for ng/mL sufficiency thresholds (≥20 ng/mL adequate per IOM; note the 30 ng/mL Endocrine-Society view as a labeled disagreement).

### P2.2 — Signs you're not getting enough sunlight

- **Intent / query cluster:** _signs you're not getting enough sun · am I getting enough sunlight · what happens if you don't get enough sun · not enough sunlight symptoms._ Broad, top-of-funnel, year-round; spikes in winter but live all year for indoor workers.
- **Primary keyword:** signs you're not getting enough sunlight
- **Secondaries:** what happens if you don't get enough sun · not enough sunlight symptoms · am I getting enough sun
- **Why we can rank:** **Soft SERP** — Reader's Digest/The Healthy, a UF physiology blog, light-therapy sellers (day-lights, Carex), Domino, an ER content page. No medical-institution wall on this exact framing, and most existing pages are thin listicles with weak sourcing. A version with real citations and a self-test framing wins both Google and LLM answers.
- **SEO/GEO:** Listicle structure is ideal for featured snippets and LLM extraction — number the signs, lead each with the sign in bold, then the mechanism. Direct-answer summary up top. FAQPage + ItemList schema. This is our strongest GEO-shaped candidate in the cluster.
- **Honest framing / Bask fit:** Hard discipline required — these signs (fatigue, low mood, frequent illness, bone/muscle aches, poor sleep, hair loss) are **non-specific and overlap with many conditions**; the post must say so explicitly and route readers to a blood test rather than self-diagnosing. Frame sunlight's role as broader than vitamin D (circadian/serotonin/sleep — links to our morning-sunlight post). Bask fit: once you suspect under-exposure, Bask quantifies how much sun you're actually getting vs. need.
- **Must-cite / verify:** Lead primary source — **Hoel & de Gruijl / "Insufficient Sun Exposure Has Become a Real Public Health Problem"** (PMC7400257) — credible, citable, and a quality signal the content farms lack. NIH ODS for the vitamin-D specifics. **Do not overstate** the myopia or hair-loss links — both are real research threads but weaker; present as "associated," not proven, or cut.

### P2.3 — Why am I vitamin D deficient if I get sun? / deficiency despite being outside

- **Intent / query cluster:** _vitamin D deficiency even though I get sun · why is my vitamin D low if I'm outside · deficient despite sun exposure · sunscreen/skin tone/glass blocking vitamin D._ Frustrated searchers who already get sun and still tested low — high engagement, very specific.
- **Primary keyword:** vitamin D deficiency despite sun exposure
- **Secondaries:** why is my vitamin D low if I get sun · vitamin D deficiency in sunny climates · reasons sun isn't raising my vitamin D
- **Why we can rank:** Genuinely under-served. Most pages answer the generic "causes" query; few directly address the "I _do_ get sun and I'm _still_ low" paradox, which is a real and counterintuitive phenomenon (deficiency is common even in sunny countries). This is an **original-angle / E-E-A-T** opportunity and a natural internal hub linking our window, sunscreen, cloudy-day, skin-type, and solar-noon posts.
- **SEO/GEO:** Listicle of reasons (glass, sunscreen, skin tone, time of day / low solar angle, AM-only exposure, latitude/season, age, body weight/BMI, GI absorption), each a liftable Q&A. Strong internal-linking hub for the whole P1 cluster. FAQPage schema.
- **Honest framing / Bask fit:** This is Bask's single most natural post — nearly every reason maps to a variable Bask tracks (UV index by time/angle, skin type, exposure window). But keep the supplement/clinician caveat for the absorption/medical reasons that sun can't fix. Honest payoff: "sometimes the fix is _better-timed_ sun; sometimes it's a supplement — here's how to tell which is you."
- **Must-cite / verify:** NIH ODS (skin tone, age, BMI/obesity sequestration, fat-malabsorption causes). Verify the "deficiency common in sunny countries" claim against a specific prevalence study before quoting a number. Reuse our own already-verified window/sunscreen/solar-noon facts for internal consistency.

**Cluster note:** Treat P2.1–P2.3 as one tightly-interlinked sub-cluster and as the _entry point_ to the broader deficiency pillar. The pure head terms (`vitamin D deficiency symptoms`, `vitamin D levels chart`, `vitamin D dosage`) remain a long-term authority play — only attempt them once this sub-cluster has earned the site some topical authority and (critically) **once GSC shows what we actually surface for.** Do not write the head terms cold.
