## Verification dossier — de-risking the open backlog

Liftable lines and caveats the Copywriter can use directly. Sources are reputable (peer-reviewed / health institutions / government) per Operating Principles.

### ✅ #4 — Does sunlight increase testosterone? (06-21 #A)

- **The study is real:** Parikh et al., _"Skin exposure to UVB light induces a skin-brain-gonad axis and sexual behavior,"_ **Cell Reports, Aug 24 2021** (PMC8411113; cell.com S2211-1247(21)01013-5).
- **Critical caveat — keep the framing honest:** the _causal_ skin→brain→gonad mechanism (via skin p53) was demonstrated **in mice.** The **human** component was a small observational cohort + questionnaires showing solar exposure **correlated** with higher testosterone and romantic passion — **not an interventional trial.** So the honest direct-answer holds exactly as drafted: _"associated, modest, and early — not proven, and burning yourself does not raise it faster."_ Do **not** let the headline imply a proven human T boost. State the mouse-vs-human distinction plainly; that honesty is the rankable gap vs. the TRT-clinic blogs.
- **Indirect (vitamin D) pathway:** present it as suggestive and **flag that several vitamin-D-and-testosterone RCTs are null** — do not overstate.

### ✅ #5 — Does a base tan protect from sunburn? (06-21 #B)

- **SPF figure verified:** a base tan provides **SPF ≈ 3 or less** (U.S. Surgeon General); some studies put it at SPF 2; tanning-bed base tans test **below SPF 1.5.** Liftable answer: _"No. A base tan is worth roughly SPF 3 or less — and the tan itself is UV damage."_ (Scientific American; CDC "A base tan is not a safe tan"; MD Anderson; UAMS Health.)
- **Stat now verified AND upgraded — timely 2026 peg:** the "~1 in 4 adults" figure traces to AAD (Axios/AAD, ~24%). **Better:** a **fresh 2026 AAD survey (released ~May 2026)** found **19% of adults** still believe a base tan prevents sunburn/reduces cancer risk, and a 2025 AAD survey found **~57% of Gen Z** believe common tanning myths. **Recommendation:** cite the **2026 AAD survey** as the lead stat — it's current, authoritative, and a freshness/GEO signal that the older affiliate pages lack. (aad.org; news-medical 2026-05-04.)
- Use AAD's own number rather than the loose "1 in 4" blog citation.

### ✅ #1 — How much vitamin D in 15 minutes of sun? (06-20 #3)

- **Holick figure verified:** full-body exposure in a bathing suit (~near-100% BSA) to **1 MED ≈ 10,000–25,000 IU** oral-equivalent vitamin D. Holick's rule: ~¼ MED over ¼ of the body ≈ **1,000 IU.**
- **Mandatory caveat — do not quote the big number bare:** the 10,000–25,000 IU figure assumes **near-full-body midday sun.** Arms-and-legs-only, lower UV, or darker skin drops it to hundreds of IU. This is precisely the "a fixed minutes number is meaningless without UV + skin type + skin area exposed" angle that justifies Bask. (Holick, _Anticancer Research_ / PMC3738435; Holick's rule reviews.)

### ✅ #3 — Do tanning beds give you vitamin D? (06-20 #5)

- **Verified:** IARC (WHO) classified UV-emitting tanning devices **Group 1 — "carcinogenic to humans"** (2009). Sunbeds emit **mostly UVA** (tans/ages skin) at high intensity; starting before age 30 raises melanoma risk by **~75%.** UVA is not the UVB that drives meaningful vitamin D. Liftable answer: _"Mostly no — tanning beds emit mainly UVA, not the UVB that makes vitamin D, and they're a Group 1 carcinogen."_ (IARC/WHO; Skcin; Woo & Eide 2010.)
- Phrase as **"most"** beds are UVA-dominant (a minority emit some UVB) — accuracy per Operating Principles.
