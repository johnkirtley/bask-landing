The most common sun exposure advice — "get 15 minutes a day" — is correct for some people and nearly useless for others. The reason is melanin: the pigment that gives skin its color also acts as a natural sunscreen, slowing the rate at which UVB converts to vitamin D. Fair skin can reach a meaningful dose in roughly 10 minutes at a UV index of 5; dark skin often needs three to four times as long for the same result.

That difference isn't a footnote. It's a central reason vitamin D deficiency is far more common in people with darker skin — especially those living at higher latitudes, where the sun's vitamin D window is already short. If you've followed the standard advice and it hasn't seemed to work, your skin type may be the explanation.

## The Fitzpatrick scale: how skin type governs vitamin D speed

Dermatologists classify skin by UV response using the Fitzpatrick scale, which runs from Type I (very fair, always burns, never tans) through Type VI (very dark, never burns). The scale tracks melanin content, which matters here because melanin absorbs and scatters UVB before it can penetrate deep enough to trigger vitamin D synthesis.

Three practical groups cover most people:

- **Fitzpatrick I–II:** Very fair to fair. Burns easily, tans little. Lowest melanin — fastest vitamin D production, highest burn risk.
- **Fitzpatrick III–IV:** Medium to olive. Sometimes burns, tans readily. Moderate melanin, moderate pace.
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** Brown to dark. Rarely or never burns. High melanin — slowest vitamin D production, longest exposure needed.

## The dark-skin vitamin D gap

The bottom row of that table is the one most sun-exposure articles skip. A person with Fitzpatrick V–VI skin at a UV index of 5 needs roughly 30 to 40 minutes — not 15. Yet the standard "15 minutes a day" recommendation was calibrated for lighter skin. When you're given someone else's number and follow it faithfully, you get someone else's results.

This mismatch has measurable public health consequences. A 2011 analysis of US NHANES data by Forrest and Stuhldreher found that 82.1% of non-Hispanic Black adults and 69.2% of Hispanic adults had serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels below 20 ng/mL, compared with 30.9% of non-Hispanic White adults. For people with darker skin who live at higher latitudes, the gap compounds: they need far more UV exposure than fair-skinned people, in a location where the vitamin D window is already shorter, weaker, and shuts down entirely in winter.

## "Pink means done" is a myth

<Callout type="warning" title="Pink isn't the finish line">
  By the time your skin turns pink, you've crossed into sunburn territory — and
  you stopped making meaningful vitamin D before that. Stop at the time, not the
  color.
</Callout>

UVB converts 7-dehydrocholesterol into previtamin D3, but continued UVB also degrades previtamin D3 into inactive photoproducts — lumisterol and tachysterol. Vitamin D synthesis plateaus on its own, well before your skin reaches its burn threshold. Burning adds no extra vitamin D. The practical rule: set a timer based on your skin type and UV index, then stop.

## What else changes your number

- **UV index** — doubles or halves your time; check yours before going out. The [UV index guide](/blog/what-uv-index-do-you-need-for-vitamin-d) explains what threshold you need.
- **Skin area exposed** — arms and legs bare is the standard baseline; face and hands alone is significantly slower
- **Season and latitude** — above roughly 35°N in winter, the UV index rarely reaches 3 and sunlight isn't a realistic vitamin D source for any skin type
- **Age** — older adults produce less 7-dehydrocholesterol, the precursor to vitamin D, and may need longer sessions or supplementation

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should someone with dark skin sit in the sun for vitamin D?**

People with Fitzpatrick V–VI (brown to dark) skin need significantly more time than the often-cited 15 minutes. At a UV index of 5, expect roughly 30 to 40 minutes with arms and legs bare. At a lower UV index of 3, the window stretches to 45 minutes or more. The exact number shifts with UV conditions, how much skin you expose, and whether you're above 35°N in winter — where sunlight isn't a realistic vitamin D source for any skin type.

**How long for fair skin to get vitamin D from the sun?**

Fair to very fair skin (Fitzpatrick I–II) makes vitamin D the fastest but also burns fastest. At a UV index of 5, around 10 to 15 minutes with arms and legs bare is typically enough. At UV 7 or higher, the window narrows to 5 to 10 minutes. Stop before any pinkness appears — that's a sign you've crossed into burn territory, not a sign the vitamin D is still accumulating.

**Does a tan mean I got vitamin D?**

Not necessarily. A tan is your skin producing melanin as a defense against UV damage — it's a protective response, not a measure of vitamin D output. You can tan from UVA-dominant light without making meaningful vitamin D (UVA doesn't drive vitamin D synthesis; UVB does), and you can make vitamin D without visible tanning. The two are related but separate processes.

**Can darker skin get enough vitamin D from the sun?**

Yes, but it takes more time and the right conditions — which are harder to meet. At higher latitudes, the vitamin D window is shorter and weaker while darker skin requires significantly more time within it. For people with Fitzpatrick V–VI skin living above 35°N, sunlight may not be a reliable year-round source; vitamin D from food and supplements becomes more important, especially in winter. A blood test is the only way to know your actual level.

**How does melanin protect against vitamin D overdose from sunlight?**

It doesn't — a separate photochemical mechanism does. Once previtamin D3 builds up in the skin, continued UVB converts the excess into inactive compounds (lumisterol and tachysterol) rather than more vitamin D. This self-limiting plateau prevents toxicity from sun exposure regardless of skin type. Melanin's role is to slow the initial rate of synthesis, not to cap the upper limit.
