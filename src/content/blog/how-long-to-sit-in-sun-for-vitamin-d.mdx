The most common sun exposure advice — "get 15 minutes a day" — is correct for some people and nearly useless for others. The reason is melanin: the same pigment that gives skin its color is also a natural sunscreen, and it slows the rate at which your skin converts UVB radiation into vitamin D. Fair skin might need 10 minutes at a UV index of 5 to make a meaningful dose; dark skin can need three to four times as long for the same result.

That gap isn't a minor footnote. It's a central reason vitamin D deficiency is far more common in people with darker skin, especially those living at higher latitudes where the sun's vitamin D window is already limited. If you've ever followed the standard advice and wondered why it doesn't seem to be working, your skin type may be the reason.

## The Fitzpatrick scale: how skin type governs vitamin D speed

Dermatologists classify skin by its response to UV exposure using the Fitzpatrick scale, which runs from Type I (very fair, always burns, never tans) through Type VI (very dark, never burns). The scale tracks melanin content — which matters for vitamin D because melanin absorbs and scatters UVB before it can penetrate deep enough to trigger synthesis.

Three practical groups cover most people:

- **Fitzpatrick I–II:** Very fair to fair. Burns easily, tans little. Lowest melanin — fastest vitamin D production, highest burn risk.
- **Fitzpatrick III–IV:** Medium to olive. Sometimes burns, tans readily. Moderate melanin, moderate pace.
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** Brown to dark. Rarely or never burns. High melanin — slowest vitamin D production, longest exposure needed.

## The dark-skin vitamin D gap: why standard advice leaves many people short

The bottom row of that table is the one most sun-exposure articles ignore. A person with Fitzpatrick V–VI skin at a UV index of 5 needs roughly 30 to 40 minutes to hit a meaningful dose — not 15. At UV 3 (the minimum threshold), that window stretches to 45 minutes or longer. Yet the standard "15 minutes a day" recommendation was calibrated for lighter skin. If you have dark skin, you're often being told a number that belongs to someone else.

The numbers show the cost of this mismatch. A 2011 analysis of US NHANES data by Forrest and Stuhldreher found that 82.1% of non-Hispanic Black adults and 69.2% of Hispanic adults had serum vitamin D levels below 20 ng/mL — the threshold most clinicians use to define deficiency — compared to 30.9% of non-Hispanic White adults. People with darker skin who live at higher latitudes (most of the US, Canada, and Europe) are fighting on two fronts: they need far more UV exposure than fair-skinned people do, in places where the vitamin D window is already short, already weak, and gone entirely in winter.

None of this is surprising. It follows directly from melanin-driven synthesis differences running into advice that was never written for them.

## "Pink means done" is a myth — and a costly one

There's a persistent belief that staying in the sun until your skin shows a slight pinkness is a useful proxy for vitamin D. It isn't, and the error is expensive. By the time your skin turns pink, you've already crossed into sunburn territory and stopped adding meaningful vitamin D.

UVB converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in your skin into previtamin D3, but continued UVB also degrades previtamin D3 into inactive photoproducts — lumisterol and tachysterol. That self-limiting feedback means synthesis plateaus well before your skin reaches its burn threshold. Pinkness is the start of an inflammatory response to UV damage. It's not a signal that synthesis has finished or that anything useful is still happening.

Burning adds zero extra vitamin D. It adds skin damage, raises long-term skin cancer risk, and, for people with darker skin, is often the wrong mental model entirely — thinking that they need to push toward visible color change to "know it's working." They don't, and they shouldn't. The goal is to reach a meaningful dose and stop, ideally before any discomfort or color change.

## What else changes your number

Skin type drives the biggest difference, but four other things shift your number enough to matter.

**UV index.** The table's columns span a 4× range in required time. Checking the UV index before you go out — not after — is the most important single habit. You need at least 3 to start the clock and the higher the number, the faster you work through your window. A low-UV spring day means longer exposure is needed; a high-UV July midday means a much shorter, more urgent session.

**How much skin you're exposing.** The times above assume bare arms and legs, which cover a meaningful fraction of body surface area. A face and hands poking out of a jacket is a much smaller target and significantly slower. At a pool or beach with most of your body uncovered, you'll reach your dose faster. More skin exposed is often the easiest lever to adjust.

**Season and latitude.** Above roughly 35° north, the sun's angle in winter is too low for UVB to reach the ground in useful amounts, regardless of your skin type. Boston, New York, Chicago, most of the UK — all sit above this line. For a person with Fitzpatrick V–VI skin at those latitudes from November to February, sunlight is not a realistic vitamin D source. Food and supplements are the path. The [main guide on how much sun you need](/blog/how-much-sun-do-you-need-for-vitamin-d) covers the latitude and season side in full.

**Age.** Skin naturally produces less 7-dehydrocholesterol with age, the raw material for vitamin D synthesis. Older adults typically need longer sessions or rely more on dietary sources to maintain adequate levels.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should someone with dark skin sit in the sun for vitamin D?**
At a UV index of 5 — a typical summer midday — Fitzpatrick V–VI skin generally needs 30 to 40 minutes with arms and legs bare. At UV 3 (the minimum), that rises to 45 to 60 minutes or more. At higher latitudes in winter, even those windows aren't achievable because the UV index never reaches 3. The specific number shifts with your exact skin tone within the range, the UV level, and how much skin you're exposing.

**How long for fair skin to get vitamin D from sun?**
At a UV index of 5, Fitzpatrick I–II skin typically needs around 10 to 15 minutes with arms and legs bare. At UV 3 it's closer to 15 to 20 minutes. Fair skin burns fastest, so the margin between a useful dose and overexposure is narrow. Stop at the time — don't wait for pinkness.

**Does a tan mean I got vitamin D?**
Not reliably. A tan is largely driven by UVA, which penetrates deeper and triggers melanin production. UVA does almost nothing for vitamin D synthesis, which is driven by UVB. Because UVA gets through the atmosphere even at low sun angles, you can tan in the morning or late afternoon while producing essentially no vitamin D. A visible tan is not proof of vitamin D production.

**Can darker skin get enough vitamin D from the sun?**
Yes, but it takes significantly more exposure time than standard advice assumes, and it requires a UV index of 3 or higher. In lower-UV seasons or at high latitudes, dark skin may simply not be able to generate a meaningful dose from sunlight alone, making supplements particularly important for people with high melanin content living far from the equator.

**How does melanin protect against vitamin D overdose from sunlight?**
Melanin slows synthesis rate, but there's also a separate skin-level self-limiting mechanism: once previtamin D3 accumulates, continued UV exposure converts the excess into inactive photoproducts rather than more vitamin D. Together these mean you cannot overdose on vitamin D from sunlight — the skin stops producing it before levels reach a toxic range. Vitamin D toxicity only comes from high-dose supplement overuse.
