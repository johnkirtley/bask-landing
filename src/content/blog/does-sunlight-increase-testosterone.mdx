# Does Sunlight Increase Testosterone? What the Research Actually Shows

A handful of observational studies have linked sun exposure to higher testosterone levels. Headlines ran with it. But when you look at the actual data, the picture is murkier than the hype suggests.

Here is what controlled studies and large cohort analyses actually found, and what that means for men trying to support healthy testosterone.

### How to read this table

- **Cross-sectional studies** measure exposure and outcome at the same point in time. They show associations, not causation. A man who spends more time outdoors may also exercise more, eat differently, or have lower body fat — all factors independently tied to testosterone.
- **RCTs** assign interventions randomly, which separates cause from correlation. The RCTs here consistently show vitamin D supplementation has little to no effect on testosterone in men who are not deficient.
- **Meta-analyses** pool results across studies. The most recent one (2024) concluded no significant effect.

The headline number — "30% higher testosterone in summer" — comes from the Lamberts study. It is real, but it was not designed to isolate sunlight as the cause.

## What you can actually do from sunlight

### 1. Vitamin D synthesis

This is the one direct, well-established hormonal effect of sunlight on the body. UVB exposure triggers vitamin D3 production in the skin. Vitamin D is involved in testosterone biosynthesis in Leydig cells, but the relationship is not linear — the enzyme that converts cholesterol to pregnenolone (the rate-limiting step) becomes saturated at normal vitamin D levels.

### 2. Indirect metabolic effects

Sun exposure correlates with outdoor activity, which supports body composition — and body composition is one of the strongest modifiable determinants of testosterone. Men with lower body fat tend to have higher testosterone. This is not sunlight doing the work; it is what people tend to do when they are outside.

### 3. Circadian rhythm regulation

Morning light exposure helps anchor circadian timing. Testosterone peaks during sleep and follows a diurnal pattern. Poor sleep quality and irregular schedules are associated with lower testosterone. Getting sunlight in the first hour after waking supports this cycle, though this is an indirect benefit.

### 4. Mood and stress

Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis. Outdoor time and sunlight reduce self-reported stress in multiple trials. This is plausible as a testosterone-supporting behavior, but no study has directly measured the cortisol-testosterone pathway through sunlight exposure.

## What the science does not support

<Callout type="warning">
  **UV light therapy does not raise testosterone.** Tanning bed studies show no significant hormonal changes. The wavelength and dosage are not comparable to natural sun exposure for vitamin D synthesis, and the thermal dose from artificial UV is different.

  **Prolonged sun exposure does not raise testosterone in a dose-dependent way.** No study has shown that spending more hours in the sun produces higher testosterone once vitamin D sufficiency is reached. The enzyme pathway is already saturated.

  **Vitamin D supplements do not raise testosterone in men who are not deficient.** This is supported by multiple RCTs and the most recent meta-analysis.
</Callout>

## What might actually help

If you are looking for evidence-based ways to support healthy testosterone:

- **Maintain a healthy body weight.** Obesity is one of the strongest predictors of low testosterone. Fat tissue contains aromatase, which converts testosterone to estrogen. Even modest weight loss (5-10% of body weight) improves hormonal profiles.
- **Sleep 7-9 hours.** Testosterone production occurs primarily during sleep. Studies show that restricting sleep to 5 hours per night for one week reduces testosterone by 10-15%.
- **Resistance training.** Resistance exercise acutely raises testosterone and, over time, improves baseline levels, particularly in men who are sedentary or overweight.
- **Correct vitamin D deficiency.** If you are deficient (below 20 ng/mL), supplementation does raise testosterone — but only back toward normal, not above it.
- **Reduce chronic stress.** Cortisol and testosterone have an inverse relationship. Chronic psychological stress lowers testosterone through HPG axis suppression.

## FAQ

### Does sunlight directly increase testosterone?

The available evidence does not support a direct, causal link between sunlight exposure and increased testosterone. Observational studies show seasonal variation in testosterone levels (higher in summer), but these studies do not isolate sunlight as the cause and do not account for confounding factors like physical activity, diet, and sleep patterns.

### How much sun do I need for vitamin D?

The amount varies by skin tone, latitude, time of day, and season. For most people in mid-latitudes, 10-30 minutes of midday sun on the arms and legs, several times per week, produces adequate vitamin D. If you cannot get regular sun exposure, vitamin D supplementation (1,000-2,000 IU/day) is a practical alternative, though it does not raise testosterone above baseline in men who are already sufficient.

### Can tanning beds boost testosterone?

No. Tanning bed use does not significantly affect testosterone levels. The UV spectrum and exposure patterns from tanning beds do not replicate the conditions needed for vitamin D synthesis, and no controlled study has found a hormonal effect.

### Does vitamin D supplementation raise testosterone?

Vitamin D supplementation raises testosterone only in men who are deficient (below 20 ng/mL). In men with normal vitamin D levels, supplementation has no significant effect on testosterone. This has been shown in multiple randomized controlled trials and confirmed in a 2024 meta-analysis of 14 RCTs.

### What is the best way to naturally support testosterone?

The strongest evidence supports maintaining a healthy body weight, sleeping 7-9 hours per night, engaging in regular resistance training, and managing chronic stress. These factors have a larger and more consistent impact on testosterone than sun exposure or vitamin D supplementation.
